FACTS ABOUT GIRLS IN INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY FIELD:
  1. While engineering and computer-related fields as some of the fastest growing fields in the United States, women only hold about one-quarter or less of those positions.
  2. U.S. women working in science and high-tech fields are 45 percent more likely than their male peers to leave the industry within a year, often due to gender bias and feelings that they are being weighed against male counterparts.
  3. The average percentage of women working in the tech industry is 30%, based on diversity reports published by 11 of the world's largest tech companies in 2014.
  4. In the mid-1980s, 37% of computer science majors were women; in 2012, 18%.
  5. Women only earn about 12% of computer science degrees nationally.
  6. Facebook COO Sheryl Sandberg, Youtube CEO Susan Wojcicki, and Yahoo! CEO Marissa Mayer were all listed as some of the most powerful Women in the Tech industry by Forbes Magazine in 2015.
  7. Asian, African American, and Hispanic women only made up 5%, 3% and 1% of the computing workforce respectively in 2014.
  8. Between 2000 and 2014, the number of first-year undergraduate women interested in majoring in Computer Science declined by 7%.
  9. In 2014, only 20% of students who took the Advanced Placement Computer Science test were women, although women comprised 56% of all Advanced Placements testtakers that year.
  10. Only about 7% of investor money goes to women-led startups.
  11. 11% of Sillicon Valley executives are women.

Enhancing girls’ futures and reducing social inequalities:

Computing jobs are among the fastest-growing and highest-paying, yet few women are benefiting from these occupations (U.S. Dept. of Labor, 2010a, 2010b). This trend potentially exacerbates existing social inequalities and barriers to girls’ future life opportunities. Increasing girls’ participation in computing is important for promoting equity and ensuring that girls are able to take advantage of these jobs and the opportunities they make possible (Barker & Aspray, 2006; Margolis, et al., 2011; Miliszewska & Sztendur, 2010; Teague, 2002)

Men have long dominated the technology industry, but the growing number of women entrepreneurs and business leaders can't be ignored. The most notable recent example is Marissa Mayer, Google's first female engineer who was appointed president and CEO of Yahoo Inc.
